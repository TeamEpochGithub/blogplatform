What is AI?

Kerem Bayraktar

Mar 09, 2022

According to Britannica, Artificial Intelligence is defined as a digital computer’s ability to perform tasks commonly associated with intelligent beings. That being said, what classifies as “intelligence”? For example, a simple program that classifies different types of flowers from images can be counted as intelligent, although computers that helped us land on the moon won’t. How do we separate a machine's knowledge to be intelligent enough from one that can do advanced machinery? Or rather, can we?

As IBM’s question-answering computer system Watson is winning the “Jeopardy!” against two human competitors, 74-times winner Ken Jenning is welcoming his new overlord. 

The foundation for intelligent machines is based on the work of Alan Turing called “Computing Machinery and Intelligence”. Alan Turing provided a framework in which he aimed to define “computational intelligence”. He defined intelligence as the ability to be indistinguishable from human behavior, rather than focusing on which features constituted intelligence. With this idea in mind, he sets the standard for artificial intelligence by developing a theoretical test called “” that has the purpose of differentiating between humans and computers. However, the modern understanding of artificial intelligence was mainly set by Stuart Russell and Peter Norvig in their textbook “Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach”. Although these definitions are rather vague and open to interpretations, we base our understanding of AI to be fitted in a mix of these 4 statements. As the broad application of AI to a plethora of problems and algorithmic solutions drastically differ, it’s difficult to pinpoint a crystal clear definition for what counts as AI and what does not. In some cases, the academic perception can even change; some of the techniques that were developed by the AI researchers, only to be reclassified as Big Data algorithms(algorithms that are used to analyze large quantities of data) later on. Therefore, this umbrella term shouldn’t be as scary as it sounds, so AI can be simplified as all efforts that aim to replicate human reasoning and behavior.

Current efforts and advancements in the field of AI can be separated into two categories of Narrow AI and General AI. General AI is the notional idea of AI in the future that would be capable of showing intelligence in the full range of cognitive abilities as good as an average human. Although it’s very exciting, it looks very difficult to reach this level of AI for at least the following few decades. Narrow AI on the other hand focuses on daily problems that we come across in our lives each day. These range from language translations to playing strategic games, and they form the basis of our current knowledge of AI in various fields. As we didn’t manage to come up with a singular knowledgeable AI that can think about answers for all of our problems (looking at you, ), current AI solutions are specifically tailored for the problem they are aiming to resolve. 

The subcategory of Narrow AI that formed the basis of many recent advancements in AI is Machine Learning. To put it simply, Machine Learning is a way of processing a body of data with various methods to try and derive a procedure that explains the data, or even predict future data. In a sense, the machine is learning how to “think and behave” from the given set of data for a more consumable and meaningful(or not!) result. The programmer in charge will choose a mathematical structure that has several decision-making rules with different parameters/specifications, which we call the “model”. These adjustable parameters will help us to fit the training data into a more convenient format that we can generalize to create the results. We will use this model in addition to something called the “objective function”, a function that will receive different data and parameters and turn it into numbers, and we will aim to optimize this result for our objective.

In recent years, further technologies were created such as Deep Learning alongside our knowledge on machine learning. Deep Learning works differently than Machine Learning in a way that it mimics the human brain structure with neurons. Deep Learning networks consist of varying amounts of layers where each layer receives input values from the previous layer and transmits output values to the next layer. Thus, further connections that can’t be seen directly from the data can be found through multilateral processing of the data. With this approach, more complex and extensive patterns can be found in the given data. A very common example for this would be Apple’s Face ID technology. As the algorithm receives more images that are captured by scanning the owner’s face, it trains itself/self-teaches for better and faster results of understanding whether the face is the owner’s or not.

The philosophical search for the definition of intelligence or its possible artificial reconstruction is still widely discussed.
