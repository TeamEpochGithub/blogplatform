AI competitions

Mar 16, 2022

A true data revolution would draw on existing and new sources of data to fully integrate statistics into decision making, promote open access to, and use of, data and ensure increased support for statistical systems. ”

As can be seen from the United Nations statement above, The importance of data science becomes more apparent every day. It will affect most of our lives in some way in the near future. It’s no surprise that the development of this field is supported via the help of computers, particularly through methods of artificial intelligence. But it’s also important to note that finding feasible and effective solutions through artificial intelligence is not an easy task of its own. And the fact that there’s no “one size fits all”  Machine Learning solution for all kinds of problems we come across everyday, directs us to these questions: What is the best way to carry the future of AI forward, without allocating too much or too less resources? How can we improve our understanding of AI for developing suitable solutions to problems that we have never faced before? The answer is AI competitions.

An AI competition in its simplest form is a contest in which individuals or teams of programmers are aiming to create fitting AI solutions within the context and the goal of the competition, which is set by the host of the competition. As an incentive, most competitions have prize systems not only limited to money but also things such as redeemable money, access to software and exposure through different means. The nature of a competition is much different than what a paid worker is doing to tackle the issue in an organizational structure which creates the main difference of the problem-solution path from what we usually come across. It invites people that are actually interested in the topic as well as to keep the competitive nature, which in return induces creative developments and state-of-the-art approaches to issues that haven’t been named before. Important to note is that the competition itself is not the goal but rather a means to an end, as the desired outcome of the competition is to bring people together for one cause and pursue the further development of the solutions that were created. 

The competitions mostly take place in competition platforms such as Kaggle, DrivenData or AICrowd. These platforms have an agreement with companies that want to host these competitions. These companies provide the resources for the hosting of the competition such as the prize pool, existing documentation and raw/processed data for the competitors if possible. A key part of most AI competitions is the fact that collaboration is not only recommended but also preferred, mostly through the hosting platform’s discussion or notebook section. This allows people to communicate with others to tackle known obstacles to save time and effort, as well as to push each other’s knowledge further by motivating and getting feedback in the meantime. At the same time, some solutions influence other people’s way of thinking and even can lead up to unconventional and creative approaches. 

Although the practices, algorithms, approaches to AI, and winning strategies change a lot from competition to competition, an established cycle for the contest is always there. Regarding the rules, the submission policy, the provided data and the definition of performance/success of the algorithm (read more about this on our blog ). Host websites such as AWS and Kaggle even take it a step further and provide some intro courses for people to start their journey through the competitions.

From supervised learning to reinforcement learning, various methods on AI are used for various competitions. So to keep things clear, we can go through some examples on how a competition takes place. The on Kaggle, the precursor of the current NFL competition that Team Epoch is competing in, is a great example for this. There is a lot of manually collected data in sports, and the NFL specifically started collecting data of each player of each game with the frequency of 10 records per second; including features such as player’s position, speed, movement etc(). Since 2017, the NFL is even providing this data to the teams so that they could analyze the plays of their players within an equal framework. What the NFL wanted in this competition was to be able to predict how far players actually can go from the data available at the time the ball carrier picks up the ball. The goal is to gain deeper insight on how the several factors do affect the flow of the play and it is very easy to see how many different applications from the solution to this can be produced if a certain amount of success can be achieved.

Players’ representation on the field. 

 For the competition, the NFL provides the necessary data with various features for training purposes that is explained thoroughly through documentation on the competition website, and expects the competitors to fit their solutions to NFL’s specific format for submission. This training data is solely for the training of the algorithms, and this is something important that we are going to visit in a later blog.  In short, training data is a dataset of which you know the input for your algorithm and you know the expected outcome. This allows the algorithm to adjust and train itself. For the competition it is also specified which method of evaluation will be used for means of transparency and clarification of performance. In addition, there’s also a “Code” section for teams to post their codes as well as a “Discussion” section for the discussion of various problems teams will come across on their journey in a controlled environment. 

As for the placement of the teams, a two-sided approach is taken. There is a public leaderboard where participants can see how well their algorithm performs on the training data, and there is a private leaderboard that the host is going to use for the final performance of all the algorithms. The leaderboard is divided to avoid overfitting the algorithm for the specific data, as the host is withholding the private dataset until all submissions are over. Some competitions achieve this by only providing 70% of their data and using the 30% for the final evaluation of the competitors, but the NFL decided to approach with a creative solution of announcing that they are going to use December’s match data for a competition that ends in November, basically making it impossible to guess. For the great technical approach of the winning team’s algorithm, you can read further on their blog on how they applied different solutions from totally unrelated problems. 

Example leaderboard from our Deep Chimpact competition.

To conclude, AI competitions are a great way to figure out problems together and develop solutions within a competition system that acknowledges the importance of knowledge sharing and pushing each other further. They are beneficial for both the competitors by providing real-life experience on real-life issues, and also provide creative solutions to organizations that seek effective results. But how do you build the right solution for such a problem? In other words, how to build and actually train the desired algorithm? And what is a competition baseline?
