AI can spot molecules on exoplanets

Kerem Bayraktar

Mar 17, 2022

As exoplanets are so far away, it has proven extremely difficult to probe their atmospheres. Research suggests that artificial intelligence (AI) may be our best bet to explore them – but only if we can show that these algorithms think in reliable, scientific ways, rather than cheating the system. 

In the future, the James Webb Space Telescope () and the Space Mission, both probes that will investigate exoplanets from their orbit in space, will help by providing high-quality spectra for thousands of exo-atmospheres. But while scientists are excited about this, the suggests it may be tricky. Due to the complex nature of atmospheres, the analysis of a single transiting planet may take days or even weeks to complete. Current research has established that . However, scientists are meticulous and sceptical, and to prove this is really the case, they want to understand how AIs think. The bad news is that AIs are terrible at explaining their own findings. Even AI experts have a hard time identifying what causes the network to provide a given explanation. This disadvantage has often prevented the adoption of AI techniques in astronomy and other scientific fields.

This is exactly what they did for an exoplanet-probing AI by “perturbing”, or changing, regions of the spectrum. By observing how the AI’s predictions on the abundances of exoplanet molecules changed (say water in the atmosphere) when each region was doctored, they started to build a “picture” of how the AI thought, such as which regions of the spectrum it used for deciding the level of water in the atmosphere. This finding provided the first method to have a sneaky peek into so-called “AI black-boxes”, allowing us to evaluate what the AIs have learnt. With these tools, researchers now can not only use AIs to speed up their analysis of exo-atmospheres, but they can also verify that their AI uses well-understood laws of nature.
